Emotional Agility by S. David

Emotional Agility by S. David

Emotional agility is a four-step approach that allows us to navigate life's twists and turns with self-acceptance, clear-sightedness, and an open mind. In her more than twenty years of research, Susan David has found that no matter how intelligent, resilient, or creative people are, when they ignore how situations or interactions make them feel, they miss opportunities to gain insight, getting hooked by thoughts, emotions, and habits that prevent them from reaching their full potential. Emotionally agile people experience the same stresses and setbacks as anyone else, but they know how to adapt, aligning their actions with their values and making small changes that lead to a life of growth.

Sustained Learning and Development means continually increasing your ability to build and maintain respectful and effective relationships with Aboriginal people. Review and reflect on how you demonstrate or don't demonstrate this competency. Consider some of the actions, such as:
  • Go to Aboriginal communities and organizations to learn about their culture and business as part of learning and development plan.
  • Seek and utilize informal learning situations responsibly.
  • Examine your own performance and seek feedback internally and externally to improve performance.
  • Integrate feedback into development plans and activities.

Claim

Circle of Courage as a Framework for Leadership

The Circle of Courage model of development was first described by by Larry Brendtro, Martin Brokenleg, and Steve Van Bockern in their book Reclaiming Youth at Risk. The model is grounded in Indigenous philosophies of child rearing and contemporary resilience research, and describes how to create an environment in which we can grow and flourish. While the model began with youth, it’s a powerful framework to support how we think about developing ourselves and others as leaders. Evaluate Your Leadership Presence
Claim

Evaluate Your Leadership Presence

Look for opportunities when you have a chance to practice your leadership presence, and have time to reflect after. When you are ready to do so, make contact with someone who will be part of the event (say, a presentation to an executive group, or facilitating a community of practice event). Tell them that you'd like them to act as a friendly observer of your presence, and that you'd like their feedback after the event. After, have a conversation with your friendly observer. What did they notice? Are your efforts succeeding? What do you want to change? What aspect of your presence do you need to focus on next? Make note, and track your progress in whatever way makes sense to you. Some people might record their reflections on video or voice, some might keep a notebook--do what works for you, but be sure to come back to your reflections to plan for your next opportunity to practice.
